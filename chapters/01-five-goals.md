# Chapter 1: The Five Goals

I wrote them down like a list of impossible things.

1. Muscle-Up
2. Full Planche
3. Handstand Push-Up
4. Front Lever
5. Pistol Squat

If you've ever set a goal that felt impossible—if you've ever looked at what others can do and wondered what they know that you don't—this is where my story starts.

Five skills. Thirteen months. And the nagging sense that I was reaching for something I had no right to chase.

---

I remember the plateau before the list.

I could do forty push-ups. Maybe five pull-ups on a good day. I'd been training consistently, showing up, building muscle. But I'd hit a wall I couldn't name.

Training felt like treading water. More reps. More sets. More variations of the same exercises that weren't taking me anywhere new. My numbers were decent. My direction was nonexistent.

The guys I watched online doing muscle-ups looked like they were floating. Planches held parallel to the ground, arms locked, faces calm. Handstands pressed up from nothing, as if gravity didn't apply to them. They seemed to understand something about their bodies that I didn't understand about mine.

I wanted that understanding. I just didn't know how to find it.

Forty push-ups meant I had endurance. Five pull-ups meant I had something to build on. But there's a phrase that kept coming up in my research: "not in straight-arm strength territory." I didn't fully understand what it meant at the time.

I had bent-arm strength—pressing, pulling, grinding through reps. But the kind of strength these skills required? The tension that holds a body horizontal to the ground, arms locked, core online? I didn't have it. I didn't even know how to train for it.

I was looking at a completely different map.

---

The gap felt impossible.

Five pull-ups to muscle-up. Forty push-ups to planche. The distance wasn't measured in reps—it was measured in something I couldn't quantify.

My first planche lean attempt lasted three seconds. Hands on the floor, leaning forward, trying to shift my weight toward my fingers—and then my wrists screamed, my arms shook, and I collapsed forward. The videos made it look like balance. In reality, it felt like controlled falling.

My first front lever tuck was worse. I hung from the bar, pulled my knees to my chest, tried to lift my hips—and my shoulder blades floated up toward my ears instead of anchoring down. I felt nothing in my lats, everything in my upper traps. My body didn't know the movement I was asking it to do.

The pistol squat humiliated me. I stood on one leg, started to lower myself, and somewhere around parallel, I lost control. My heel popped off the ground. My hip shot backward. My extended leg couldn't stay straight. I collapsed at the bottom like a broken hinge.

And then there was the muscle-up.

I jumped to the top of a bar, grabbed the handles, and tried to lower myself slowly through the transition—that magic zone between pull-up and dip where the muscle-up happens. My shoulders gave out immediately. Not slow failure—instant collapse. I dropped into the dip position with no control, elbows flaring, chest caving.

I tried it again. Same result. I had enough strength to do pull-ups. I had enough strength to do dips. But the transition between them? My body had no idea what to do.

I wasn't sure it was possible. Could someone who starts with five pull-ups actually do a muscle-up in a year? Could someone who'd never held a planche lean actually hold a full planche? The timeline felt ambitious. The gap felt insurmountable.

---

I watched tutorials. I collected cues.

Pull your elbows in. Depress your shoulders. Squeeze harder. Fire your lats. Keep your core tight. Every video gave me something new to think about, and none of it stuck.

The cues piled up in my head like unread mail—more noise, no clarity. I'd try to implement one thing, forget another, overcorrect on a third. My movements got worse, not better. Trying to think about everything meant I couldn't feel anything.

I tried what I knew: grinding.

More pull-ups. More push-ups. More time hanging from the bar, hoping strength would translate. Week after week, I felt the same walls. My planche lean still collapsed after seconds. My muscle-up transition still caved. My pistol squat still fell apart at the bottom.

I was working hard. I wasn't working smart. And I didn't know the difference yet.

The question kept nagging: was I doing this wrong? Was there something fundamental I was missing? The people who could do these skills—did they know something I didn't? Or were they just stronger?

I didn't have the answer. But I had the suspicion that strength wasn't the whole story.

---

The shift wasn't a technique. It was a decision.

I stopped chasing numbers.

Not because numbers didn't matter, but because they weren't telling me anything useful. Forty push-ups didn't explain why my planche lean collapsed. Five pull-ups didn't explain why my muscle-up transition felt impossible. The metrics I'd been tracking measured output, not capability.

I needed to track something different.

Video became my mirror. I filmed everything—every set, every attempt, every failure. Not to post online. To see what I couldn't feel. The gaps became obvious on camera. Shoulder blades floating when I thought they were locked. Ribs flaring when I thought I was tight. Angles wrong when they felt right.

The footage didn't lie. My body was doing things I didn't know it was doing. And until I could see them, I couldn't fix them.

I built a system. Three tracks—Push Mastery, Pull Mastery, Leg Mastery—rotating weekly so nothing overtrained. Each session had purpose beyond just getting tired. Each movement had intention beyond just completing reps.

**Skills, not reps.** That became the operating principle.

The goal wasn't to do more. The goal was to *learn*. To figure out what my body needed to figure out. To close the gap between what I could do and what these skills demanded.

I didn't know yet that alignment wasn't something you force—it's something you allow. I didn't know that my skeleton was the foundation, not my muscles. But I knew that trying harder hadn't worked.

And I was ready to try different.

---

I didn't know what these goals would teach me.

I thought I was training for skills. Muscle-up. Planche. Handstand push-up. Front lever. Pistol squat. Five boxes to check.

What I didn't know: each skill was a question.

The pistol squat would ask why my ankle had been popping for years—and show me that the answer wasn't stretching. The planche would ask why my shoulders always felt like they were fighting me—and show me that scapular depression wasn't about forcing my shoulders down. The muscle-up would ask why my transition felt impossible—and show me that motor patterns mattered more than strength.

Each skill would unlock something beyond itself.

But I'm getting ahead of myself. That understanding came later—much later. At this point, all I had was a list. Five goals. A timeline. And a growing suspicion that the path forward wasn't going to be what I expected.

I had my goals. I had my timeline.

What I didn't have yet was an understanding of how wrong my approach would turn out to be—and how that wrongness would become the most valuable part of the journey.
