# Chapter 1: The Rhythm

Gravity never takes a day off.

It's pulling right now — on your shoulders, your hips, your spine. It pulled yesterday when you felt great. It's pulling today even if you're tired, tight, stressed, or not in the mood. It'll pull tomorrow regardless of what you do tonight.

The ground pushes back. Always. That's the deal. Two forces, constant, and your body caught in the middle — hundreds of variables shifting between them.

Some days you're warm and loose. Some days you're cold and creaky. Some days you slept eight hours; some days you got four. Some days you want to train. Some days you'd rather do anything else.

The forces don't care. Gravity pulls. Ground pushes. Your body has to figure it out.

---

For years I thought consistency meant fighting the variables.

Three workouts a week. No excuses. If I missed a day, I'd try to make it up. If I missed a week, I'd feel like I was starting over. The goal was to override the variability — to show up the same way every time, regardless of how I felt.

It didn't work.

Life isn't consistent. Work gets crazy. Sleep gets disrupted. Motivation comes and goes. Some weeks I'd hit all three workouts and feel untouchable. Other weeks I'd miss one, then another, then look up and realize a month had passed.

And every time I "fell off," I'd restart. Back to workout one. Back to the beginning. The cycle of starting over became its own pattern — more familiar than the training itself.

I thought the problem was discipline. I just needed to try harder. Show up more. Override the variables with willpower.

But willpower has a ceiling. And variables don't stop.

---

The shift came when I stopped thinking about frequency and started thinking about sequence.

I have three workouts. Always the same three. They rotate: 1, 2, 3. 1, 2, 3.

That's it. That's the whole system.

But here's what changed everything: I never break the order.

If I only do one workout this week, it's workout 1. Next week, if I only do one, it's workout 2. The week after, workout 3. Then back to 1.

Some weeks I do all three. Some weeks I do one. Some weeks — honestly — I do none. But when I come back, I pick up wherever the sequence left off. I don't restart. I don't "make up" missed sessions. I just continue the rhythm.

The tempo changes. The rhythm doesn't.

---

This sounds simple. It is simple. But it changed everything.

Because now there's no such thing as "falling off."

A slow week isn't a failure. It's just a slower beat. The sequence is still intact. The rhythm is still running. I'm not starting over; I'm just picking up the next note.

One workout in two weeks? Fine. That's one beat in a longer measure. The music doesn't stop — it just stretches.

Three workouts in four days? Also fine. That's a faster tempo. Same rhythm, different speed.

The variables do their thing. Cold days. Tired days. Travel. Stress. Life. And the rhythm keeps running underneath all of it — patient, unbroken, always ready for the next beat whenever I can show up.

---

This is what consistency actually looks like when life is variable.

Not perfect attendance. Not "no excuses." Not overriding your body's signals with willpower.

Just: never break the sequence.

Find your rhythm. Protect it. Let the tempo breathe.

---

Here's what I've learned about the variables.

They're not obstacles. They're the weather.

Cold and tight? That's today's weather. You don't argue with weather. You dress for it. Warm up longer. Move slower. Find organization in *this* climate, not the one you wanted.

Tired and unmotivated? Same thing. The weather is lousy today. So the session is shorter. The intensity drops. But the beat still happens. You show up, do what you can, and the rhythm continues.

The goal isn't to feel the same way every session. The goal is to find organization *regardless* of how you feel.

Some days organization comes easy — you're warm, rested, focused, and the positions just click. 

Some days you have to hunt for it — you're stiff, distracted, your shoulder is cranky, and nothing feels right. Those days, you spend more time searching. You adjust. You find a version of the movement that works for today's body, today's conditions.

Both days count. Both days build something.

The easy days build capacity. The hard days build adaptability.

And adaptability is the real skill.

---

Gravity pulls down. Ground pushes up.

Your body is the mediator — a living negotiation between two constant forces, running through a system that changes every single day.

The skeleton stacks or it doesn't. The joints trust load or they protect. The muscles transmit force or they fight. And all of this shifts based on variables you can't fully control.

What you *can* control is the rhythm.

Show up. Find organization in whatever state you're in. Let the tempo flex while the sequence holds.

1, 2, 3. 1, 2, 3.

Some weeks fast. Some weeks slow. But always the next beat, never a restart.

For some bodies, the measures stretch even longer — weeks, sometimes months, between beats. That's still rhythm. That's still the sequence holding.

That's how you build confidence in your own adaptability. Not by mastering perfect conditions, but by learning to find organization in any condition.

The forces are constant. You are variable. The rhythm is what ties them together.

---

The five skills I'm training — pistol squat, muscle-up, handstand push-up, front lever, planche — they're not the point.

They're training grounds.

Each one forces the body to solve the gravity-ground equation in a different orientation, with different constraints. Inverted. Single-leg. Horizontal. Pushing. Pulling. Rotating.

The skill isn't the planche. The skill is learning to find organization when you're cold, tired, leaning forward, and your shoulders want to shrug up. The planche is just the context where you practice that.

The skill isn't the pistol squat. The skill is learning to find organization when your ankle is stiff, your hip is tight, and you're descending on one leg with nowhere to hide. The pistol is just the context.

Same adaptability, different puzzles.

And once you start developing that adaptability — once your body learns that it can find organization across variable conditions — something shifts.

You stop being afraid of bad days.

You stop waiting for perfect conditions.

You start trusting yourself to figure it out, whatever "it" is, whatever state you're in.

That's the capability I didn't have six months ago. Not a planche. Not a front lever. Something deeper.

Confidence that I can meet gravity and ground, in any condition, and find my way to organized.

---

That's what this book is about.

Not five skills. Not a training program. Not "here's how I learned to planche."

It's about the relationship between constants and variables. Between gravity and ground and the living, changing, adapting body caught between them.

It's about finding your rhythm, protecting it, and letting it carry you through the days when everything else is chaos.

And it's about what happens when you stop fighting the variables and start learning from them — when the hard days become the most valuable training days, because that's where adaptability lives.

Find the rhythm. Protect the sequence. Let the tempo breathe.

The forces are constant. You just have to keep showing up.
