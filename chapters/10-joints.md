# Chapter 10: Taking the Brakes Off

I'd had that pop in my ankle for years.

Every step, a click. Every squat, a grind. It wasn't painful, just *there*—a constant reminder that something wasn't quite right. I'd tried everything: stretching, yoga, ankle circles, foot massage, calf massage. I'd roll my calves until they were tender, hold stretches until my legs shook, do all the mobility drills the internet told me to do.

If you've ever stretched endlessly and wondered why the tightness always comes back—this chapter explains what finally worked.

Nothing stuck. The tightness always came back. The pop never left.

Then I started training pistol squats.

---

I wasn't training pistols to fix my ankle. I was training them because they were one of my five goals—the one that seemed most approachable. But somewhere around week eight, I noticed something strange.

The pop was quieter.

Not gone. But smoother. Less grinding, more gliding. The ankle that had clicked with every step for as long as I could remember was suddenly... easier. Moving like it was supposed to.

I didn't change my stretching. I didn't add any new mobility drills. I just kept loading the ankle through full range, over and over, every time I dropped into a single-leg squat.

---

The realization came later: I'd been trying to *stretch* my way to mobility. But stretching doesn't teach the nervous system anything.

Most long-term tightness isn't a flexibility problem. It's a trust problem. The nervous system looks at a joint position and thinks: *I don't feel stable here. Keep the muscles tight to protect it.* So you stretch, and the muscles relax temporarily, and then they tighten right back up because the underlying problem—the lack of stability in that range—was never addressed.

What actually works is *loading* the range. Not forcing it, not stretching it—using it. Under control. Repeatedly. Until the nervous system learns: *this range is safe.*

That's what pistol squats did for my ankle. Deep dorsiflexion. Tibia moving forward over the foot. The whole ankle-knee-hip chain loading together. Every rep was a message: *we can handle this.*

So the body removed the brakes.

---

The hamstrings released too. And the hips. Tightness I'd carried for years started letting go—not because I stretched harder, but because I trained through the ranges I'd been avoiding.

It was a shift I didn't expect: skill work *is* the mobility work. The movements themselves were teaching my joints to move. Not just strengthening muscles—reorganizing the whole system.

---

The warmups changed everything.

I started paying attention to what my joints were telling me before I even touched the movements. And that's when I noticed the asymmetry.

During a simple shoulder warmup, I could feel it: the right side moved smoothly, with control. The left side felt like impingement—a sense of grinding, of things catching, of the joint not quite trusting itself.

Same movement. Two different experiences.

That wasn't a strength gap. It was a *control* gap. My right shoulder knew how to organize itself through that range. My left shoulder was still protecting, still compensating, still holding tension against positions it didn't trust.

Once I saw it, I couldn't unsee it. Warmups stopped being something I rushed through. They became diagnostic. A way to check in: *what's moving freely today? What's still guarded?*

---

The wrist prep became non-negotiable.

Every push day, before I touched planche work or handstand holds, I'd run through the same sequence: wrist circles, palm pulses, fist rocks. Two minutes of preparation that made everything after it cleaner.

But it wasn't just protection. It was activation. The wrist work woke up the joint, reminded the nervous system that we were about to load it, gave everything downstream a chance to organize.

We don't push through wrists. We prepare them. That became a rule.

---

The pattern repeated everywhere.

Ankle work improved my surfing—board control felt more natural even after weeks away from the water. Hip mobility freed up my running. Shoulder prep sharpened my planche leans.

It wasn't that the joints got more flexible. They got more *organized*. The nervous system stopped treating every range like a threat and started treating it like terrain it knew how to navigate.

That's the progression that actually works: not passive mobility, but active control. Not stretching, but strength through range.

---

Most people stay stuck trying to stretch their way to stability. I did too, for years. The ankle kept popping. The hamstrings stayed tight. The shoulders never felt quite right.

What changed wasn't my flexibility. It was my approach.

Load the joint. Train the range. Let the nervous system learn.

The tightness releases on its own.

Same ankle. Same hamstrings. Same shoulders.

Different relationship to movement.

The joints were unlocked. But there was still one problem I kept running into—the old patterns that crept back under load. Breaking those would require something different.

Organized, not forced.
