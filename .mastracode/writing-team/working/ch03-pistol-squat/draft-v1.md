# Chapter 3: The Pistol Squat

I stood up from the bottom without grabbing anything.

No wall. No pole. No rings to steady myself. Just my left leg, the floor, and a balance I'd never had before.

I did it again. And again. Multiple sets. Not lucky reps—*real* reps.

The bottom of a pistol squat is where most people fail. Your butt drops below your knee, your ankle runs out of range, your pelvis tilts, and you either fall backward or fight your way up. I'd lived in that fight for months.

But this time, I didn't fight. I sat into the bottom like it was a position, not a fall. My ankle stayed stacked. My knee tracked forward. My hip held.

Something had changed.

My right leg was close too—I could feel it engaging, finding the pattern—but it still had a sticking point right at the bottom. When my hips dropped below my knee, I wanted to hinge forward. The left had figured something out that the right was still learning.

Three months earlier, I couldn't do a single pistol without holding onto something.

---

The pistol squat was one of five goals I'd written down at the start of the year. Muscle-up. Full planche. Handstand push-up. Front lever. Pistol squat.

At the time, I could do forty push-ups and maybe five pull-ups. I had a base, but these skills felt like a different language. The kind of thing other people did—athletes, gymnasts, people who'd been training since childhood.

The pistol seemed like the most approachable. One leg. Squat down. Stand up. How hard could it be?

Hard.

My first attempts were wall-assisted, gripping a doorframe like it might save my life. Five reps on the first set, then failing by rep four on the second and third. My legs weren't weak—I'd squatted plenty. But something about going down on one leg exposed holes I didn't know I had.

The progression was supposed to be clean: box pistols, counterweight pistols, assisted pistols, negatives, then full pistols. I worked through it. But even as I got stronger, something kept catching me at the bottom.

And my right ankle? It had been popping for years. Every time I circled it. Every time I went down stairs. A grinding, clicking sound that never hurt but never went away. I'd tried everything: stretching, yoga, ankle circles, foot massage, calf massage. Years of it.

Nothing worked.

---

The sticking point was always the same. Bottom third of the squat.

"You're losing tension right at the bottom," the feedback said. And I could feel it. The moment my hips dropped below my knee, something let go. Not strength—*organization*. My pelvis would tilt back, what they call a butt wink. My ankle would collapse slightly inward. And instead of sitting into the bottom, I was falling into it and catching myself.

The hinge pattern was the tell. When I hit depth, my body wanted to fold forward instead of stack vertically. The right side was worse. I'd feel like I needed to pitch my torso toward my knee just to not tip backward.

I tried to fix it the way I'd tried to fix everything: more effort, more reps, more stretching.

Stretch the hip flexors. Stretch the calves. Do ankle circles. Do more mobility work before training.

Years of this approach. Years of stretching, yoga, ankle circles, foot massage, calf massage.

And still the ankle popped. Still the bottom of the pistol felt like a battle.

The wall wasn't strength. I could do the reps. The wall was somewhere else—somewhere I couldn't stretch my way past.

---

Then I stopped trying to fix the joint.

I started *using* it.

That's when everything shifted. I'd been approaching mobility like a problem to solve before training could happen. Loosen the ankle, then squat. Stretch the hip, then move. The joint was broken, and I needed to repair it before I could load it.

But the breakthrough came from the opposite direction.

**Loading the joint through movement taught the nervous system what years of stretching couldn't.**

I started focusing on the mechanics of the skill itself. Not fixing the ankle first, but using the pistol to teach the ankle how to work under load.

And the ankle began to heal.

"I've had a popping in my ankle for years," I noted after one session. "But since working on dorsiflexion and pistols, the popping is starting to ease. Still there, but it hasn't been this smooth in a while."

This was almost more impressive than the pistol itself. I'd tried stretching, yoga, ankle circles, foot massage, calf massage—*for years*. None of it touched whatever was causing the popping.

But training single-leg squats? Actually loading the joint through its range with control? That's what finally helped.

The realization hit: most tightness isn't about length. It's about the nervous system protecting joints that don't feel stable. When I taught the joint to be stable under load, the nervous system stopped putting on the brakes.

"Taking the brakes off." That's how I started thinking about it.

The cue that landed was simple: "Own the bottom, don't escape it."

Instead of falling into the lowest position and fighting my way out, I sat into it. Owned it. Made it a position I could control, not a hole I had to climb out of. And suddenly the bottom wasn't the enemy. It was the training.

---

The pistol wasn't just fixing my squat. It was fixing everything downstream.

Two weeks after I noticed the ankle smoothing out, I went surfing. I hadn't been in the water for three weeks—long enough that I expected to feel rusty, awkward, out of sync with the waves.

Instead, I felt locked in.

I was able to go out in three-to-four foot surf and catch large waves. And in the moments that used to throw me off—adjusting on a steep face, recovering from a bad pop-up, holding my balance when the board shifted unexpectedly—I stayed centered.

"My pistols specifically help with my balance in precarious spots," I realized.

The connection made sense. A pistol squat trains the entire ankle-knee-hip control system. Foot stability into ankle dorsiflexion into knee tracking into hip stability into core balance. That same chain fires every time you stand on a surfboard.

People think pistols are just a quad exercise. But they're training something much deeper: explosive control through your ankles. The hidden superpower of the movement.

And once that chain wakes up, it doesn't just work in the gym.

I could feel my foot grabbing the ground differently now. Standing on one leg, I felt connected to the floor in a way I hadn't before. The intrinsic muscles of my foot were turning on, coordinating with my ankle, feeding into my hip.

My hamstrings and hips started releasing tightness I'd carried for years. Not from stretching—from *moving*. The nervous system was letting go of protective tension because it finally felt stable.

---

The pistol wasn't a party trick.

It was teaching me how my lower body was supposed to work all along. Not through flexibility work. Not through passive stretching. Through loading, through control, through owning positions I used to avoid.

And once it worked in the gym, it worked everywhere.

Same ankle. Same knee. Same hip.

Different relationship between them.

Organized, not forced.
