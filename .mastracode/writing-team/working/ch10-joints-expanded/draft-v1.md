# Chapter 10: Taking the Brakes Off

The ankle moved easier.

I was dropping into another pistol squat, week eight of training, and somewhere in the descent I noticed it: less grinding. Less catching. The pop that had been there for years—the one that accompanied every step, every squat, every awkward ankle roll—was quieter.

If you've ever spent years stretching, rolling, massaging, and still felt tight—if you've wondered why mobility work never seems to stick—this chapter explains what finally worked.

I didn't stretch my ankle that morning. I didn't foam roll my calves. I just kept loading it through the same range, over and over, every rep of every pistol squat.

And something was changing.

---

I'd had that pop in my ankle for as long as I could remember.

Not painful. Not disabling. Just *there*. A constant low-grade reminder that something wasn't quite right. Every step, a click. Every squat, a grind. The kind of thing you eventually stop noticing because it never goes away.

I tried everything.

Stretching: I'd hold my calves in a doorway stretch until my legs shook, counting the seconds, convinced that if I could just hold it long enough, the tightness would release.

Yoga: Twice a week for months. Downward dog, warrior poses, all the hip openers. My hamstrings felt looser for an hour after class, and then they didn't.

Ankle circles: Twenty each direction before every workout. The joint would loosen up, move a little smoother, and by the next morning it was right back where it started.

Foot massage: Tennis ball under the arch, rolling out the fascia. It felt good in the moment. The pop didn't care.

Calf massage: Foam roller until the tissue was tender. I'd read that tight calves limit dorsiflexion, so I attacked them like they were the enemy. Nothing changed.

The pattern was maddening. I'd put in the work—the stretching, the rolling, the mobilizing—and for a few hours I'd feel different. Then I'd wake up the next day and the tightness was back. The pop was back. Like my body had a reset button that erased every session.

For years, this was just how it was. Some people have creaky ankles. I was one of them.

---

The pistol squats weren't supposed to fix my ankle.

They were just one of my five goals—the one that seemed most approachable. I wasn't thinking about mobility. I was thinking about strength, about single-leg control, about getting that first unassisted rep.

But somewhere around week three, I started noticing asymmetries I'd never paid attention to before.

During a set of shrimp squats—the warmup drill I'd been doing to prep for pistols—I felt it clearly: my left quad activated cleanly. Sharp, responsive, fully connected. My right quad felt... dumb. Delayed. Like the signal from my brain was taking a detour before reaching the muscle.

This wasn't a strength gap. Both legs could produce similar force. It was a neural activation gap. My brain was better at finding my left quad than my right.

Once I noticed it, I couldn't un-notice it.

My coach explained the pattern: dominant-leg compensation. Most right-footed people push off their right leg in daily life, but they stabilize with the left. This creates overactive stabilizers on one side and underactive prime movers on the other.

It wasn't about one leg being stronger than the other. It was about my brain having a clearer map of one side than the other. The left leg had a better mind-muscle connection. The right leg was operating on a delay.

Slight hip alignment differences. Ankle mobility differences. Even a five percent difference in how one ankle dorsiflexes changes how smooth the whole movement feels. The good news, my coach said, was that this was the easiest imbalance to fix—if you catch it early. And I was catching it early.

The fix was simple: start every leg day with the weaker side first. Train right leg while fresh. Let left leg work second. Over time, the neural activation gap would close.

I didn't believe it at first. The gap felt like it was about muscle, about strength, about something structural. But within two weeks of focusing on right-leg-first warmups, I could feel the quad activating more cleanly. The "dumb" feeling started to fade.

That was the first hint that what I'd been thinking of as physical problems were actually neural problems.

And I'd been training for months without seeing any of this.

---

The warmups became diagnostic.

I started paying attention to what my joints told me before I touched the actual skills. And that's when the real asymmetry showed up.

During a simple shoulder warmup—just slow circles, feeling the range—I could tell immediately: the right side moved smoothly. Controlled. The joint tracked like it was supposed to.

The left side felt like impingement. Grinding. Catching. Something not quite right. Like the shoulder didn't fully trust itself in that position.

Same movement. Same warmup. Two completely different experiences.

My coach named it: "That's not just tightness. That's a control gap, not a strength gap."

The left side wasn't weak. It was protecting. Compensating. The nervous system had decided that range wasn't safe, so it created tension, impingement sensations, protective stiffness.

The right side had learned to control that range. The left side hadn't.

The difference wasn't strength. Both shoulders could produce similar force. It was trust. The right shoulder's nervous system said: "I know how to organize this range. Go ahead." The left shoulder's nervous system said: "I don't trust this position yet. Let me hold some tension just in case."

And here was the part that rewired everything: you can't stretch your way out of a control gap. Passive mobility doesn't teach control. You can't convince a nervous system to trust a range by forcing it into that range. You have to earn the trust through repeated, controlled exposure.

I'd been treating a trust problem as a flexibility problem for years—and wondering why nothing worked.

---

The realization came in pieces.

First: most long-term tightness isn't a flexibility problem. It's a protection response.

Second: the nervous system's logic is brutally simple. "If I don't trust this position, I'll keep the muscles tight to protect the joint." That's not dysfunction. That's doing its job.

Third: stretching addresses tissue length, not stability. You can stretch a range all day, but if the nervous system doesn't trust that range, it will tighten right back up.

The question wasn't "how do I get more flexible?" The question was "how do I teach my nervous system that this range is safe?"

And the answer was loading.

Not forcing. Not stretching. Loading. Using the range under control, repeatedly, until the joint proved it could handle it.

That's what pistol squats had been doing for my ankle. Every rep: deep dorsiflexion. Tibia moving forward over the foot. Stabilizers activating. The whole ankle-knee-hip chain loading together through a range I'd been avoiding for years.

The mechanism made sense now. Chronic ankle popping usually comes from a few things: the talus joint not gliding smoothly, tight calves limiting dorsiflexion, weak stabilizers in the foot and ankle, lack of controlled loading through the range.

Pistols fixed all four at once. Deep dorsiflexion. Tibia moving over the foot. Foot stabilizers activating. Joint capsule getting repeated, controlled motion. Over time, the joint surfaces start tracking properly again. The popping decreases because the joint is finally moving the way it was designed to move.

Each rep was a message: *we can handle this.*

So the body removed the brakes.

The hamstrings released too. And the hips. Tightness I'd carried for years started letting go—not because I stretched harder, but because I trained through the ranges I'd been avoiding. The pistols and shrimp squats improved hip capsule mobility, posterior chain coordination, glute activation. When the glutes started working properly, the hamstrings could finally stop overworking to compensate.

The same pattern, everywhere I looked.

---

The wrist prep became part of this same pattern.

Every push day, before planche work or handstand holds, I'd run through the sequence: wrist circles, twenty each direction. Palm pulses on the floor, ten reps. Fist rocks, ten reps. Back-of-hand stretch, just ten seconds.

Two minutes. Non-negotiable.

At first I thought this was about protection—don't hurt your wrists doing straight-arm work. But it was more than that.

The prep was activation. Wake up the joint. Remind the nervous system that we're about to load it. Give everything downstream a chance to organize before we ask for force.

On days when I skipped the prep—when I was rushed, when I figured I'd warm up as I went—the first few sets felt different. Stiffer. More resistant. Like the joint was suspicious of what I was asking it to do.

On days when I did the prep properly, the work after felt cleaner. More connected. The wrists weren't fighting the movement; they were transmitting it.

We don't push through wrists. We prepare them. That became a rule.

---

The pattern repeated everywhere I looked.

The ankle work didn't just fix my ankle. It improved my surfing. I went out in three-to-four-foot surf after a two-week break and caught large waves, my balance more natural than before. The board felt more stable under my feet. Pop-ups were quicker. And I hadn't practiced surfing at all—I'd been training pistol squats.

Surfing demands ankle dorsiflexion, hip rotation, knee tracking, rapid balance corrections. Single-leg squats train all of those at once. I'd upgraded my movement system, not just my strength. The pattern was portable.

The hip mobility that came from single-leg work freed up my running. The shoulder prep sharpened my planche leans. Everything was connected.

And it made sense now. These weren't separate improvements. They were all the same thing: the nervous system learning to trust ranges it had been protecting.

**Active mobility under load teaches the nervous system what passive stretching cannot.**

That's the principle that changed everything. Not more flexibility. Better control. Not forcing ranges open. Teaching them.

Skill work is the mobility work. The movements themselves were teaching my joints to move. Not just strengthening muscles—reorganizing the whole system.

---

The warmups stopped being warmups.

They became check-ins. Daily diagnostics. A way to ask: what's moving freely today? What's still guarded? Where does the nervous system trust me, and where is it still protecting?

The wrist circles would tell me if my push day would be smooth or grippy. The shoulder circles would reveal if the left side was still guarding. The ankle rotations would show if yesterday's pistol work had done its job.

The answers varied. Some days everything felt smooth. Other days, the left shoulder would catch again, or the right hip would feel sticky, or one ankle would be stiffer than the other. Sleep, stress, hydration, how hard I'd trained the day before—all of it showed up in the warmup.

But now I had a framework. If something felt guarded, I didn't try to force it. I loaded it gently, gave the nervous system evidence that the range was safe, and let it adapt. The goal wasn't to make the guarding go away immediately. The goal was to earn trust over time.

Most people stay stuck trying to stretch their way to stability. I did too, for years. The ankle kept popping. The hamstrings stayed tight. The shoulders never felt quite right.

What changed wasn't my flexibility. It was my approach.

Load the joint. Train the range. Let the nervous system learn.

The tightness releases on its own.

---

Same ankle. Same hamstrings. Same shoulders.

Different relationship to movement.

The joints were unlocked. But there was still one thing I kept running into—the patterns that crept back under fatigue, the compensations that reappeared when I wasn't paying attention.

Breaking those would require a different approach entirely.

Organized, not forced.
