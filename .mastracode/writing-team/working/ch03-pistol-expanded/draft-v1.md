# Chapter 3: The Pistol Squat

I stood up from the bottom without grabbing anything.

That was the moment. Not the depth—I'd been hitting depth for weeks. Not the balance—that had been building. The difference was that I *trusted* it. My left side dropped into the hole and came back up like it belonged there. No hesitation. No bailout. No reaching for the wall.

If you've ever felt stuck at the bottom of a squat—or wondered why stretching never fixes anything—you know the wall I'm talking about.

I did another rep. Then another. Multiple sets, unassisted, on a leg that had been shaky just weeks earlier.

My right side still had that hinge—that moment where my hips wanted to shoot back instead of staying stacked. But my left side was *there*. The pattern had locked in.

---

A month earlier, I couldn't do this.

My first real pistol session was humbling. Wall-assisted, both hands lightly touching for balance. I did five reps on the first set with decent range of motion. Felt solid. Then sets two and three hit, and I failed on rep four. Both legs.

Not because my quads gave out. Because something else did.

Stability fatigue, my coach called it. The supporting foot, the glutes, the hip stabilizers—they all fatigue fast in a pistol squat. Most people quit at rep two or three. I made it to four. But that fourth rep exposed the gap: I had base strength and mobility, but I was missing repeatability. The bottom third of the squat was where everything fell apart.

That sticking point at the bottom wasn't a muscle problem. It was a coordination problem.

And my left side was slightly better than my right—even then. During shrimp squats that same week, I noticed I could activate my left quad cleanly, but my right felt dumb. Delayed. Like my brain couldn't find it as easily. Not a strength gap. A neural one.

That asymmetry would follow me for weeks.

---

Session after session, I showed up and hit the same walls.

Week two: I added slow negatives after failing. Three to four seconds down, using the wall lightly for balance. The idea was to build eccentric control—to teach my body that the bottom was safe. Some days it worked. Other days, my right quad still felt like it was half asleep.

Week three: I tried counterbalance pistols with a light weight held in front. The counterweight shifted my center of mass forward, removing some of the fear reflex. Suddenly my right side could descend without the hinge. But the moment I dropped the weight, the pattern came back.

Week four: My coach had me do bottom-range hovers—lower into the pistol with assistance, stop one to two inches above the bottom, hold for three seconds, then stand back up. This was where the work got honest. My right side would shake. My left side was stable. Same body, two different experiences.

Progress wasn't linear. Good days, bad days. One session I'd hit clean reps; the next session I'd fall back into old patterns. The only thing that stayed consistent was showing up.

---

The real struggle wasn't the reps. It was the pattern.

On my right side, something strange happened every time my hips dropped below my knee. Instead of staying stacked—knee forward, torso tall, weight through the heel—my body wanted to hinge. Shift the load backward. Dump into my glute and hip instead of driving through my quad.

My coach explained it clearly: "Your right side doesn't trust the knee-forward position yet."

That was exactly what it felt like. Trust. My left side had learned to trust the bottom. My right side was still scared of it.

The hinge was a protection pattern. When the nervous system doesn't trust a position, it finds a workaround. Mine was to unload the quad at the hardest part of the movement—right when I needed it most.

The cue that started to help: *Knee forward early, then sit straight down.*

Not "push hard." Not "don't fall." Just: knee forward, sit between the hips. If the knee led, the hinge disappeared.

But knowing the cue and owning the cue were different things. For weeks, I'd feel the right pattern on a good rep, then lose it on the next one. The left side was building. The right side was catching up, slowly.

---

The bottom range was its own problem.

Even when I kept the knee forward, I'd hit the bottom of the pistol and feel a micro-hesitation. Down... pause... up. Instead of smooth. Down, stable, up.

My coach diagnosed it: "Hip flexor and deep glute coordination mismatch. You're losing tension right at the bottom."

The fix was deceptively simple: bottom pause pistols. Three sets of three, each leg. Pause two to three seconds at the bottom.

The cue: *Own the bottom, don't escape it.*

That cue changed how I thought about the movement. I'd been treating the bottom like something to survive—get through it fast, stand up before you fall. But the pause forced me to *be* there. To find stability in the position instead of rushing past it.

Hip stability. Ankle control. Real strength—not momentum disguised as strength.

The first time I paused at the bottom without wobbling, I knew something had shifted. It wasn't dramatic. Just a quiet moment where my body stopped fighting the position and started owning it.

---

But the deepest struggle was older than the pistol itself.

For years, I'd had a pop in my right ankle. Every step, a click. Every squat, a grind. Not painful, just *there*—a constant reminder that something wasn't quite right.

I'd tried everything. Stretching. Yoga. Ankle circles. Foot massage. Calf massage. I'd roll my calves until they were tender, hold stretches until my legs shook, do every mobility drill the internet told me to do.

Nothing stuck. The tightness always came back. The pop never left.

Same story with my hamstrings. Same story with my hips. Tight, always tight, no matter how much I stretched.

I didn't understand why until the pistols started working.

---

The shift came when I realized I'd been solving the wrong problem.

Stretching affects tissue length. Temporarily. But most long-term tightness isn't a flexibility problem. It's a *trust* problem.

The nervous system looks at a joint position and thinks: *I don't feel stable here. Keep the muscles tight to protect it.*

So you stretch, and the muscles relax temporarily, and then they tighten right back up—because the underlying problem was never addressed. The joint still doesn't feel safe in that range.

What actually works is *loading* the range. Not forcing it. Not stretching it. Using it. Under control. Repeatedly. Until the nervous system learns: this range is safe.

That's what pistol squats did for my ankle. Deep dorsiflexion. Tibia moving forward over the foot. The whole ankle-knee-hip chain loading together. Every rep was a signal: *we can handle this.*

So the body removed the brakes.

**Active mobility under load teaches the nervous system what passive stretching cannot.**

Around week eight of pistol training, I noticed something strange. The pop in my ankle was quieter. Not gone—but smoother. Less grinding, more gliding. The ankle that had clicked with every step for years was suddenly... easier.

I hadn't changed my stretching. I hadn't added new mobility drills. I'd just kept loading the ankle through full range, over and over, every time I dropped into a single-leg squat.

Years of trying to fix the problem passively. Weeks of loading it actively. That was the difference.

The hamstrings released too. And the hips. Tightness I'd carried for years started letting go—not because I stretched harder, but because I'd finally trained through the ranges I'd been avoiding.

I'd been trying to stretch my way to stability. The pistol taught me to *load* my way there.

Skill work *is* the mobility work.

---

A few weeks later, the carryover showed up where I least expected it.

I hadn't surfed in two or three weeks. The swell was solid—three to four feet, fast-moving. I paddled out not knowing how my body would feel.

First wave, I caught it clean. Dropped in, stayed centered, made the section.

Then another. Then another.

Something had changed.

When the board dropped unexpectedly, I didn't scramble. When I got caught off-axis, my body corrected before I had to think about it. Those precarious spots—the moments where you're one micro-adjustment away from eating it—felt *different*. More automatic. Less desperate.

The pistol squat had trained exactly that pattern:

Ankle adjusts. Knee tracks. Hip stabilizes. Core counters.

The same chain I'd been building on land was showing up in the water. My brain knew how to correct balance before I fell.

I wasn't stronger. I'd been strong enough to surf for years. But the pattern was finally connected.

---

The pistol taught me what my ankle needed to learn. But my shoulder was a different problem entirely—one that would take months to untangle.

Same ankle. Same knee. Same hip.

Different relationship between them.

Organized, not forced.
