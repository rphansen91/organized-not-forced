# Chapter 7: The Planche

---

I set up for another pike pushup. Feet on the bench, hands on the floor, hips high. I'd done hundreds of these by now.

But this time, something shifted.

I wasn't pressing. I was letting. Letting my ribs settle. Letting my shoulder blades find the ribcage underneath them. Letting gravity do what I'd been trying to force with muscle.

My shoulders didn't fight. They landed.

I pressed up. Smooth. Not grinding—gliding. The same movement I'd been struggling with for months suddenly felt like it belonged to me.

I did another rep. Same thing. Another. Still there.

Effortless but strong.

That's when I understood what I'd been doing wrong the entire time.

---

Three months earlier, I couldn't hold a planche lean without my shoulders screaming.

The progression was supposed to be simple: lean forward until your shoulders stack over your wrists, hold that. Build the straight-arm strength. Add load over time.

In theory.

In practice, I'd get into the lean position and immediately feel my upper traps fire. My neck would tighten. My shoulder blades would lift away from my ribs like they were trying to escape.

"Depress harder," I told myself. Push the shoulders down. Keep them pinned.

So I'd squeeze. Force. Grind.

Six reps on the first set. Four on the second. By the third, I was dropping to my knees to finish the last two. Pike pushups were supposed to be a stepping stone to handstand pushups, but every session felt like I was pushing against myself.

Everyone said planche takes the longest. They weren't wrong—but they didn't mention why. It wasn't about strength. It was about something I couldn't yet name.

---

The pattern was always the same.

I'd set up. Lean forward. And somewhere in the first rep, the connection would break. Not dramatically—just a subtle shift. My left side would feel different from my right. My shoulder blade would float away from my ribs. My biceps would start working overtime to stabilize what my scapula couldn't hold.

Upper trap grabs. Bicep compensates. Neck tightens. Scap floats.

I called it "the disconnected feeling," because that's exactly what it was. Like the signal between my brain and my shoulder was getting lost somewhere along the way. Power was being generated, but it wasn't arriving where it needed to go.

Force bleeding out.

The harder I tried to depress my shoulders—to "jam them down" like every cue I'd ever heard—the worse it got. The muscle was there. The effort was there. But the output wasn't matching the input.

Something was fundamentally off in how I was approaching the movement.

---

The shift happened in pike position.

I wasn't even thinking about it. I set up, started the rep, and instead of forcing my shoulders down, I just... didn't.

I let the angle of my body do the work. Let gravity pull my torso toward the floor. Let my ribs settle into place.

And my scapulae landed.

Not because I muscled them there. Because the position allowed it.

**Depression is not "jam shoulders down."**

**Depression is allowing the torso and scap to organize under load.**

That reframe changed everything.

The old cue was "depress harder." Push down. Force it. The new cue was simpler: *Let gravity stack me into position.*

When I stopped forcing, something unexpected happened. My ribs stacked. My scap connected. My lats—which had always felt like they were working independently—suddenly became part of a chain. Ground to torso to scapula to arm to force.

For the first time, I felt what efficient force transfer actually meant. Not more effort. Better organization.

**Strength is force expressed through organization.**

That's why gymnasts look effortless. Not because they're trying harder. Because their structure carries load efficiently. Less wasted tension. Cleaner leverage. Better force transfer. More repeatability.

I'd touched that feeling. And it explained why months of grinding hadn't gotten me there—grinding was the problem.

---

A few weeks later, I went surfing.

I hadn't been out in almost three weeks. The swell was solid—three to four feet, fast-moving closeouts. The kind of waves where you need to pop up quick or you're eating sand.

I caught my first wave. It jacked up fast. I compressed, planted my hands, and pressed.

My body showed up.

Shoulders didn't collapse. Core stayed tight. Weight stayed forward. I drove force into the board instead of scrambling to catch up with the wave. Channeled straight into the face.

Planche mechanics under chaos.

It wasn't that I was stronger. I'd been strong enough to surf for years. It was that the pattern was finally connected. The same organization I'd found in pike position—ribs stacked, scap supported, force transferring instead of bleeding—showed up automatically when I needed it.

That's what this training is supposed to do. Not just build strength. Build a body that shows up when it matters.

Same ocean. Same board.

Different relationship to load.

Organized, not forced.
