# Chapter 7: The Planche

I set up for another pike pushup. Feet on the bench, hands on the floor, hips high. I'd done hundreds of these by now.

But this time, something shifted.

If you've ever felt like your shoulders were fighting you instead of helping you—like the harder you tried to hold them down, the more they wanted to lift away—you know this frustration.

I wasn't pressing. I was letting. Letting my ribs settle. Letting my shoulder blades find the ribcage underneath them. Letting gravity do what I'd been trying to force with muscle.

My shoulders didn't fight. They landed.

I pressed up. Smooth. Not grinding—gliding. The same movement I'd been struggling with for months suddenly felt like it belonged to me.

I did another rep. Same feeling. Another. Still there.

Effortless but strong.

I wasn't even stronger that day. I was better organized.

---

Months earlier, I couldn't hold a planche lean without my shoulders screaming.

The progression was supposed to be simple: lean forward until your shoulders stack over your wrists, hold that position, build the straight-arm strength. Add load over time.

In theory.

In practice, I'd get into the lean position and immediately feel my upper traps fire. My neck would tighten. My shoulder blades would lift away from my ribs, floating instead of settling.

"Depress harder," I told myself. Push the shoulders down. Keep them pinned.

So I'd squeeze. Force. Grind.

Six reps on the first set. Four on the second. By the third, I was dropping to my knees to finish the last two. Pike pushups were supposed to be a stepping stone to handstand pushups, but every session felt like I was pushing against myself.

The cues I'd collected from YouTube, Reddit, and coaching videos all said the same thing: depress your shoulders. Pull them down and back. Keep them away from your ears.

So that's what I did. I jammed them down. I squeezed harder.

Everyone said planche takes the longest. They weren't lying. But they didn't explain why. It wasn't about strength—I could feel that much. My shoulders were getting stronger. I could hold longer, lean further. But the pattern wasn't clicking.

The frustration wasn't physical. It was conceptual. I was working hard, but the results weren't proportional to the effort. Something about the approach itself was wrong. I just couldn't see what.

---

For the next few weeks, the pattern repeated.

I'd set up for planche leans. Lean forward until the weight shifted onto my hands. And somewhere between comfortable and too far, my arms would start to shake.

The shaking wasn't weakness. It was neural recruitment, my coach told me. Straight-arm positions stress the anterior delts, the long head of triceps, the connective tissue around the elbows. They don't fail smoothly—they tremor first.

I learned to back off when the tremor started. Find the edge, then pull back slightly. Work inside the range where I had control.

The protocol became: sets one and two at my most controllable lean. No shaking. Perfect balance. Twenty to thirty seconds. Set three was where I'd explore—lean slightly deeper, let the toes just start to de-weight, hold for a second or two, then come back. Touch the edge, don't live there.

On one session, my third set, I felt something new: my toes started to de-weight. Just a flicker. The load shifted forward onto my hands, and for a split second, I wasn't supporting my legs anymore.

And then it vanished.

I couldn't sustain it. The moment I tried to hold that position, the shaking intensified, my form broke, and I was back to square one.

The micro-floats came and went. Glimpses of planche that disappeared as fast as they arrived. You couldn't force them. You could only create the conditions where they might show up, and sometimes they did, and mostly they didn't.

After those sessions, I'd move to lean pushups. The pattern there was similar—reps would drop off fast. Five on the first set, fail on six. Three on the second, fail on four. I was staying in a true planche lean, not a watered-down version, and the cost was rapid fatigue. The quality was there, but so was the struggle.

---

Around week four, I started noticing something else.

The connection would break mid-rep. Not dramatically—just a subtle shift where one side felt different from the other. My left shoulder blade would float away from my ribs. My biceps would start working overtime to stabilize what my scapula couldn't hold.

I named it "the disconnected feeling."

Upper trap grabs. Bicep compensates. Neck tightens. Scap floats.

That's what was happening on almost every rep. I could generate force, but it wasn't arriving where it needed to go. I'd start a set feeling organized, and by rep four or five, something would shift. The left side would feel hollow, empty, like I was hanging on my joints instead of pushing through them.

Force bleeding out.

I was pushing, but the push was leaking through the gaps in my structure. Power generated didn't equal power transmitted. Somewhere between my hands and my shoulders, energy was escaping.

The asymmetry was maddening. My right side would feel solid, connected. My left side would feel loose, compensating, fighting for stability. It wasn't a strength imbalance—both sides could produce similar force. It was a coordination gap. The left side didn't know how to organize under load the way the right side did.

The harder I tried to depress my shoulders—to "jam them down" like every cue I'd ever heard—the worse it got. The muscle was there. The effort was there. But the output wasn't matching the input.

I watched videos of gymnasts holding planche. They looked calm. Organized. Not grinding against themselves. I tried to copy their positions, but copying a position doesn't give you the sensation of being organized. It just gives you a shape without the architecture.

I'd finish sessions feeling like I'd worked hard but accomplished nothing. My reps were getting sloppier, not cleaner. The pattern wasn't improving.

Something was fundamentally wrong about how I was approaching the movement.

---

Week six, my coach dropped an insight that changed everything.

"Your scapula rides on your ribcage."

Simple sentence. Everything changed.

If the ribcage flares—scap control becomes unstable. If the ribcage twists—scap follows. If the ribcage disconnects from the core—the shoulder blade has nothing to land on.

I'd been trying to control my shoulders while ignoring the platform they sat on.

Think about what that means. Every time I tried to "depress harder," I was pulling my shoulders down onto an unstable surface. The scapula had nowhere to go. No foundation to settle on. So it floated. And when it floated, everything else compensated.

The compensation pattern wasn't muscular. It was structural. My left side was trying to depress the shoulder without torso support. So the upper trap grabbed. The bicep stabilized. The neck tightened. The scap floated.

All those months of fighting to hold my shoulders down—I'd been fighting against my own structure. You can't force a scapula to settle when the ribcage underneath it is flaring.

I wasn't weak. I was organized wrong.

The reframe was subtle but total: instead of "pull shoulders down," think "stack ribs, connect torso, shoulders settle."

Depression wasn't an action I performed. It was a position I allowed.

---

The shift happened in pike position.

I wasn't even thinking about it. I set up, started the rep, and instead of forcing my shoulders down, I just... didn't.

I let the angle of my body do the work. Let gravity pull my torso toward the floor. Let my ribs settle into place.

And my scapulae landed.

Not because I muscled them there. Because the position allowed it.

The sensation was completely different. Before, I'd been trying to force depression with lats and traps, jamming the shoulders down. Now I felt the whole torso supporting the shoulder. The difference was night and day—smooth instead of forced.

**Depression is not "jam shoulders down."**

**Depression is allowing the torso and scap to organize under load.**

That reframe changed everything.

The old cue was "depress harder." Push down. Force it. The new cue was simpler: *Let gravity stack me into position.*

When I stopped forcing, something unexpected happened. My ribs stacked. My scap connected. My lats—which had always felt like they were working independently—suddenly became part of a chain. Ground to torso to scapula to arm to force.

No energy was being lost anymore. No tension bleeding out through the gaps. The force I generated was the force that arrived.

For the first time, I felt what efficient force transfer actually meant. Not more effort. Better organization.

I did another rep to make sure it wasn't a fluke. Same feeling. Another. Still there. The pattern was real.

**Strength is force expressed through organization.**

That's why gymnasts look effortless. Not because they're trying harder. Because their structure carries load efficiently. Less wasted tension. Cleaner leverage. Better force transfer. More repeatability.

I'd touched that feeling. And it explained why months of grinding hadn't gotten me there—grinding was the problem. I'd been trying harder when I should have been organizing better.

---

A few weeks later, I went surfing.

I hadn't been out in almost three weeks. The swell was solid—three to four feet, fast-moving closeouts. The kind of waves where you need to pop up quick or you're eating sand.

I caught my first wave. It jacked up fast. I compressed, planted my hands, and pressed.

My body showed up.

Shoulders didn't collapse. Core stayed tight. Weight stayed forward. I drove force into the board instead of scrambling to catch up with the wave. The push channeled straight into the face.

This exact structure—depressed and stable scap, connected hollow, forward projection—was literally my pop-up mechanics. I hadn't practiced surfing pop-ups. I'd practiced force transfer in pike position. And the pattern translated.

Planche mechanics under chaos.

It wasn't that I was stronger. I'd been strong enough to surf for years. It was that the pattern was finally connected. The same organization I'd found in pike position—ribs stacked, scap supported, force transferring instead of bleeding—showed up automatically when I needed it.

No wonder surfing suddenly felt different. The carryover wasn't about arm strength or shoulder endurance. It was about how force moved through my body. When the torso is organized, when the scap is supported, when the chain is connected—force transfers instantly instead of leaking out.

That's what this training is supposed to do. Not just build strength. Build a body that shows up when it matters.

Same ocean. Same board.

Different relationship to load.

The waves didn't know I'd been training planche. My body didn't announce that it had reorganized. But the results were there anyway. The pattern was portable. What I'd learned in one context showed up automatically in another.

Force transfer wasn't just about the pike. It was a principle that would reshape how I thought about every movement—especially the ones I'd been ignoring.

But there was one area where I'd been ignoring the same lesson for years. A fundamental gap in my training that no amount of skill work could compensate for. The next chapter would force me to confront it.

Organized, not forced.
