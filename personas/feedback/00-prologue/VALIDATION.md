## REVISION-001: Replace vague "click" with mechanical specificity

### Original Text
> "And then something clicked. Not a joint. Not a pop. A *connection*."

### Revised Text
> "And then I felt my ribcage drop. Not collapse—settle. My shoulder blades, which had been floating somewhere behind me, stopped fighting and pressed into my ribcage. For the first time, my upper body felt like one piece instead of a stack of parts."

---

### Marcus (Frustrated Practitioner)

**Original Concern:**
> "'Something clicked.' — I've read this phrase in every transformation story ever. What specifically clicked? Was it a position? A thought? A sensation? The click is the whole point and you're glossing over it."

**Assessment: IMPROVED ✓**

This is what I wanted. "Ribcage drop" and "shoulder blades pressed into ribcage" are things I can actually feel for. "Stack of parts" finally describes what disorganized FEELS like — which means I can recognize it in myself.

The original was a tease. This delivers.

**New concerns:** None. Don't overthink it.

---

### Elena (Movement Nerd)

**Original Concern:**
> "The 'click' language — somatic experiences are real, but describing them without mechanism risks sounding like every yoga teacher who says 'just breathe into the space.' What actually happened in your tissue?"

**Assessment: IMPROVED ✓**

Better. "Shoulder blades pressed into ribcage" describes scapulothoracic positioning without being clinical. "Floating somewhere behind me" is actually a good description of scapular winging/dyskinesis in lay terms.

"Stack of parts" is a decent metaphor for lack of tensegral integration.

I still want more mechanism eventually, but for a prologue hook, this is appropriate depth.

**New concerns:** The phrase "stopped fighting" is slightly anthropomorphizing the scapulae, but it's forgivable in experiential writing.

---

### Derek (Skeptical Athlete)

**Assessment: IMPROVED ✓**

Okay, "ribcage drop" and "shoulder blades pressed into ribcage" — that's concrete. I can picture that. "Stack of parts" actually makes sense for what a bad overhead position feels like.

**New concerns:** None for this section.

## Post-Validation Notes

### What Worked
- Mechanical specificity ("ribcage drop," "shoulder blades pressed into ribcage") landed universally
- Explaining the mechanism of gravity in one sentence was sufficient
- Reframing surfing as one example among many defused the "flex" read
- Small inclusion language ("forty push-ups or four") made a big difference for accessibility

### What to Watch
- Elena noted "stopped fighting" anthropomorphizes the scapulae — acceptable for now but don't overdo this style
- Rachel noted the inclusion sentences slightly interrupt flow — acceptable tradeoff but keep an eye on pacing

### Learnings for Future Chapters
- One sentence of mechanism after a poetic insight is usually enough
- Universal examples (getting out of a chair) should precede niche examples (surfing)
- Explicit inclusion language is cheap and effective — use it early
